Case Question

Revenue is growing — so why are margins deteriorating year over year?

The Superstore is posting consistent top-line growth across all regions. But profitability is trending in the wrong direction. The question is whether this is a category mix problem, a discounting problem, or both — and which levers actually move margin.

Initial Hypothesis

Hypothesis 1.0

Margin compression is driven by aggressive discounting across all categories — a company-wide pricing discipline issue rather than category-specific performance.

Methodology

01P&L decomposition — broken revenue, cost, and profit down by category, sub-category, region, and year
02Discount correlation analysis — plotted discount depth against margin at the order level to isolate causality
03Category benchmarking — compared margin profiles across Technology, Office Supplies, and Furniture
042018 sales forecast — linear regression on 4-year trend, validated with seasonal decomposition

Key Findings

Primary Finding

Furniture is structurally unprofitable — and growing it is making things worse.

Tables at −8.6% margin on $200K+ revenue. The initial hypothesis was wrong — discounting amplifies the problem, but category-specific structural loss is the root cause.

Discounting amplifies, doesn't cause it — orders above 30% discount average −12% margin. Furniture + high discount is the worst combination visible on the scatter.

Technology is the margin anchor — 17–19% consistently. Copiers and Phones carry minimal discount pressure and are the only sub-categories worth scaling.

Central region underperforms on profit despite comparable revenue — likely highest discount concentration outside Furniture.

Office Supplies shows the recovery template — SKU rationalization in 2016–2017 lifted margin. Furniture needs the same treatment.

Updated Hypothesis

↑ Hypothesis Revised

Margin compression is category-specific, not company-wide. Furniture is structurally loss-making — and volume growth in that category is actively eroding total profitability. Discounting amplifies the problem but is not the root cause. The fix is category restructuring, not blanket pricing discipline.
