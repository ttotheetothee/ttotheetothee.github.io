Case Question

Can we identify high-risk members before a stroke occurs — and translate model output into a business intervention?

Strokes are one of the highest-cost events in a health plan. The question is whether population-level risk factors can predict individual stroke risk well enough to justify a proactive intervention program — and what that program is worth to a mid-sized insurer.

Initial Hypothesis

Hypothesis 1.0

Age is the dominant predictor of stroke risk. A simple age-stratified intervention targeting members over 65 would capture most of the preventable cases.

Methodology

01Class imbalance handling — 4.2% stroke prevalence required stratified train/test split and class_weight=‘balanced’ in both models
02Feature engineering — BMI-age interaction terms, hypertension × diabetes compound risk flag, 5 binned age buckets
03Dual model evaluation — Logistic Regression for interpretability, Random Forest for predictive power; compared on ROC-AUC, Precision, Recall, F1
04Business translation — mapped model score percentiles to 3-tier member stratification and estimated avoided claims at 15% intervention effectiveness

Key Findings

Primary Finding

ROC-AUC 0.86

Random Forest outperforms Logistic Regression (0.78). The gap proves non-linear comorbidity interactions are real — a simple age rule can’t capture them.

Top predictors: age, hypertension, heart disease, BMI×age interaction — age alone is necessary but not sufficient

Hypertension + diabetes elevates stroke probability 3.4× vs. neither condition — the compound flag is the most actionable signal

Age-only stratification would miss ~38% of high-risk members under 60 with compound comorbidities

False positive cost at Tier 1 is low relative to avoided claims — $800 wellness outreach vs. $85K–$120K avg stroke event

Business Interpretation — 3-Tier Member Stratification

Tier 1 — High Risk

~15K

Model score ≥ 0.7. Immediate care management: nurse outreach, medication adherence, quarterly screenings. Estimated 5,000+ stroke-risk individuals in a 500K population.

Tier 2 — Moderate Risk

~45K

Model score 0.4–0.7. Preventive engagement: digital wellness programs, annual cardiometabolic panels, lifestyle coaching incentives. Intercept before risk elevation.

Tier 3 — Standard

~440K

Score < 0.4. Standard preventive benefits apply. Monitor for score migration annually — early detection of tier drift is the second-order value of the model.

Intervention Value Model — 500K-Member Insurer

15% intervention effectiveness assumption · $100K avg stroke claim · $800 outreach per Tier 1 member

↑ Hypothesis Revised

Hypothesis Revised

Age is a necessary but insufficient predictor. Compound comorbidity (hypertension + diabetes + BMI) is the actionable risk signal. A model-driven stratification outperforms age-only targeting by 38% on high-risk capture rate.

Recommendations

Three horizons: deploy scores into care management now, validate with an RCT pilot, then extend the infrastructure to a full comorbidity suite.
