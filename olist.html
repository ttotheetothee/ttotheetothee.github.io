Case Question

Is Olist's fulfillment process breaking down — and if so, where and for whom?

The platform is experiencing rising dissatisfaction signals. The question is whether it's a seller problem, a carrier problem, or a structural infrastructure problem — and which regions bear the brunt of it.

Initial Hypothesis

Hypothesis 1.0 — Pre-analysis

Competitive pressure from Mercado Livre may be driving seller churn and declining service quality — sellers migrating to a better platform, leaving weaker operators behind.

Methodology

01Joined 8 datasets — customers, sellers, orders, items, payments, reviews, geolocation, products (96K+ rows)
02Calculated late delivery rates by state and seller using estimated vs. actual delivery timestamps
03Correlated delay rates with review scores to quantify customer dissatisfaction per delivery failure
04Isolated high-volume problem sellers — late rate above 50% combined with avg review score below 3.0

Key Findings

5 high-volume sellers have late rates above 50% and avg review scores below 3.0 — concentrated failure pattern

Late deliveries concentrated in Northeast Brazil — AL, MA, SE, PI, CE, BA, PA lead in late-order rate

Developed states also affected: RJ at 11.6% late — points to carrier behavior, not just remote geography

Carrier identified as root cause — failure pattern maps to carrier routes, not seller profiles or categories

Seller churn and Mercado Livre displacement could not be confirmed — dataset limitation, not absence of effect

Updated Hypothesis

↑ Hypothesis Revised

Logistics failure confirmed as the primary driver of customer dissatisfaction. Carrier performance in hard-to-reach regions is the root cause — not seller quality, not competitive pressure. The Mercado Livre hypothesis was set aside due to insufficient comparative data.
